                    <div class="prose">
                        <p>Each chapter of <strong>Good Strategy/Bad Strategy</strong> opens a window onto a different aspect of strategy. It is not a rehash of existing strategy doctrines and frameworks. Rather, it presents views on a range of issues that are fundamental yet have not been given much daylight.</p>
                        <ul>
                            <li>Good Strategy has a basic underlying logic: coherent action backed up by an argument, an effective mixture of thought and action. I call this basic underlying structure the <em>kernel</em>.
                            </li>
                            <li>Good Strategy is rare. Many organizations that claim to have a strategy do not. Instead, they have a set of financial performance goals. Or, worse, a set of vague aspirations.</li>
                            <li>Bad strategy occurs when there is bad doctrine, when hard choices are avoided, and/or when leaders are unwilling or unable to define and explain the true nature of the challenge being faced.</li>
                        </ul>
                        <p>In <strong>Good Strategy/Bad Strategy</strong> I have tried to write a book that is <em>interesting</em>, presenting fascinating stories and new ways of looking at things. In addition, I have tried to write an <em>honest</em> book about strategy. In particular, I wanted to honor the fact that how a strategist discovers a decisive objective and creates advantage lies at the very edge of our understanding, something only glimpsed out of the corner of the mind.</p>
