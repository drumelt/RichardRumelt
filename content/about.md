        <p>I am often asked for guidance on how to become an expert in strategy, or a strategy consultant or speaker. My first response is that it is a little like rock-and-roll—you mostly have to write the songs you sing.</p>
        <p>My own path was circuitous: electrical engineering at UC Berkeley; spacecraft design at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory; a prize-winning doctoral dissertation at Harvard Business School; three years founding and teaching at the Harvard-sponsored business school in Iran; then forty years at UCLA Anderson School of Management, with three of those years spent at INSEAD in France. Along the way, I developed my own viewpoint and voice—one that challenges conventional wisdom about what good strategy actually is.</p>
        <p>Much of what I have learned about strategy has come from working with executives, which I have done continuously throughout my career. Whether at the Department of Defense, a small start-up, or a giant enterprise like Shell, the best have a knack for teasing out the key elements of a situation and then focusing energy and resources on resolving problems or seizing opportunities. I have also seen too many leaders who mistake vague generalities about ambitions and purposes for strategy, or who treat it as an exercise in setting financial goals. My most rewarding work is when I can help an executive or group sort through the buzzing confusion of their situation and identify the crux issues they can and should address. There's a moment when you can see it click—and suddenly the path forward is clear.</p>
        <p>Today I live in Boise, Idaho, with my wife Kate and dog Jamie. When I am not writing, speaking, or consulting, I find time to ski, hike, camp, toss balls to Jamie, and build an AI "Strategy Oracle."</p>
